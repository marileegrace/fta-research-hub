Executive Summary & Investment Thesis

▾
AST SpaceMobile is a binary, asymmetric infrastructure bet on owning the only LEO constellation purpose-built to deliver true cellular broadband — not just texts — to ordinary phones. After a ~38% drawdown from its May all-time high, the market is over-weighting two recoverable setbacks (a lost satellite, a rocket explosion) and under-weighting an FCC-blessed 248-satellite franchise, ~45 MHz of dedicated spectrum, and equity from both AT&T and Verizon. We are cautiously bullish.

ASTS trades at roughly $82.41 (June 14, 2026), down from a $133.86 all-time high set May 28, with a market capitalization near $32B across its multi-class share structure. This is a pre-commercial company: Q1 2026 revenue was just $14.7M against a $191.0M net loss. The entire valuation rests on a single question — can it deploy and operate the constellation before the capital, the spectrum window, or the carriers' patience runs out?

The bull case is structural. AST holds FCC commercial authorization (April 2026) to operate 248 satellites delivering Supplemental Coverage from Space using premium 700/800 MHz low-band spectrum coordinated with AT&T, Verizon and FirstNet, plus up to 45 MHz of lower mid-band via the Ligado deal. It is targeting ~45 satellites in orbit by year-end 2026 and carries a war chest of ~$3.5B in cash against >$1.2B of committed backlog. No competitor — including SpaceX's Starlink Direct-to-Cell — currently offers genuine broadband (video, data) to a standard handset.

The bear case is equally real: this remains a cash-incinerating, pre-revenue hardware program facing relentless dilution, a brutal launch-cadence dependency, and a deep-pocketed SpaceX competitor. Conviction is therefore moderate — sized as a high-volatility, asymmetric position, not a core holding.

Microeconomic & Valuation Analysis

▾

The Addressable Market

The TAM is the global mobile subscriber base — roughly 5.5–6 billion handsets — overlaid on the ~90% of Earth's surface that lacks terrestrial cellular coverage (oceans, deserts, rural interiors). Direct-to-device satellite connectivity is projected to grow from a few hundred million dollars today to a multi-tens-of-billions market by the early 2030s, with sell-side CAGR estimates frequently in the 30–40% range. AST's wholesale model — sharing revenue with mobile network operators rather than acquiring subscribers — lets it address that base without building a retail brand.

Valuation Posture

On forward 2026 revenue guidance of $150–200M, ASTS trades around 180x forward sales — firmly in the "Euphoric" band of the P/S scale (>30). On trailing 2025 revenue of $70.9M the multiple is absurd; on a 2028+ "constellation complete, continuous coverage" revenue base of several billion, today's price could look cheap. This is the definition of a real-option valuation: you are not buying current cash flows, you are buying the discounted probability of a future utility-scale network. A conventional P/E is meaningless — the company is deeply loss-making.

Qualitative — Moat, Management & Culture

▾

The Moat — Thinking Systemically

AST's moat is a stack of compounding scarcities, not a single feature. First, physics & IP: the BlueBird arrays are the largest commercial communications structures ever flown (BlueBird 6 spans ~2,400 sq ft), and the phased-array beamforming that lets a satellite close a link with a 0.1-watt phone is protected by a deep patent estate. Second, spectrum: through carrier partnerships AST gets premium 700/800 MHz low-band for free, plus ~45 MHz of mid-band from Ligado — a regulatory asset competitors cannot simply buy. Third, regulatory: FCC authorization for 248 satellites and SCS service is a multi-year, hard-won barrier. Fourth, distribution: exclusive-leaning relationships with the carriers controlling billions of existing subscribers. Each layer is individually defensible; stacked, they form a genuine systemic moat — provided execution delivers.

Management & Capital Allocation

ASTS is founder-led: Abel Avellan is founder, chairman and CEO, with a prior exit (Emerging Markets Communications, sold to Global Eagle) demonstrating he can build and monetize satellite-comms businesses. Capital allocation is aggressive and creative — the team has repeatedly tapped convertibles and at-the-market equity to fund the constellation, and in Feb 2026 opportunistically refinanced higher-coupon 2032 notes with cheaper long-dated 2036 paper. The flip side is chronic dilution and a balance sheet increasingly layered with convertible debt.

Culture & Organizational Traits

The culture reads as engineering-first and mission-driven, with high failure tolerance baked in — necessary for an aerospace hardware company where rockets explode and satellites de-orbit. Talent density is concentrated in RF/phased-array and aerospace engineering. The risk is key-person dependence on Avellan and a decision-making structure that is centralized rather than an idea-meritocracy. Innovation pace is high; the constraint is manufacturing throughput and launch access, not ideas.

Moat verdict: Wide but unproven. The combination of spectrum + regulatory + carrier distribution is close to un-replicable. But a moat around a network that isn't yet operational is potential energy, not kinetic — it only converts to durable economics once continuous coverage is live.

Risks & Anti-Thesis — Steelmanning the Bear

▾

If I were short ASTS, here is the case I would press. Each risk is engaged seriously, with probability, impact, and whether it is already discounted in the price.

1. Execution & launch-cadence dependency

The entire thesis requires launching dozens of large, complex satellites on a 1–2 month cadence using third-party rockets (SpaceX, Blue Origin). The BlueBird 7 loss and the New Glenn anomaly show how fragile this is. A multi-month cadence slip pushes continuous coverage into 2027 and forces renegotiation of carrier service-level commitments. This is the single most likely thesis-breaker.

2. Relentless dilution & capital intensity

AST has funded itself with serial convertible notes and ATM equity. Each raise dilutes holders and layers in conversion overhang (~11.9M potential shares from the Feb 2026 note alone). A full constellation may cost billions more than currently funded. The market's reflex to every financing is "more shares = lower value."

3. SpaceX / Starlink competition

Starlink Direct-to-Cell, paired with T-Mobile's "T-Satellite," is already live for messaging and benefits from SpaceX's owned, near-free launch capacity and capital depth. T-Mobile's Starlink exclusivity expires in 2026 — if SpaceX matures broadband D2C, AT&T and Verizon could be tempted to defect. AST's edge is broadband-grade capacity to unmodified phones, but the competitive clock is loud.

4. Valuation / expectations risk

At ~180x forward sales and ~$32B market cap on $14.7M quarterly revenue, the stock embeds near-flawless execution. Any disappointment — a missed launch, a guidance trim, a carrier delay — triggers violent 20–40% drawdowns (as just witnessed). The downside in a "show me" market is structurally large.

5. Regulatory / spectrum interference disputes

The Ligado mid-band acquisition faces opposition (Iridium has urged the FCC to block it over interference/coordination concerns). Spectrum coordination across borders and incumbents is slow and contestable. A regulatory setback would directly impair a core moat asset.

Glossary & Methodology

▾

Definitions for hyperlinked terms used above. Each links back to where it first appeared.

FCC Commercial Authorization / SCS

The U.S. Federal Communications Commission's grant permitting a satellite operator to provide service commercially. AST's April 2026 order approved a 248-satellite constellation for Supplemental Coverage from Space (SCS) — using terrestrial mobile spectrum from space to fill coverage gaps. Why it matters: regulatory approval is a multi-year, hard-to-replicate barrier to entry. ↩ back to Section 1

Supplemental Coverage from Space (SCS)

A regulatory framework allowing satellites to use a partner carrier's licensed terrestrial spectrum (e.g., 700/800 MHz) to reach standard phones where there is no cell tower. Why it matters: it lets AST deliver service to unmodified handsets using premium low-band spectrum it doesn't have to buy. ↩ back to Section 1

TAM — Total Addressable Market

The total revenue opportunity if a product captured 100% of demand. Formula: (# potential customers) × (avg revenue per customer). Why it matters: frames the ceiling for a growth thesis — here, billions of phones × global coverage gaps. ↩ back to Section 2

CAGR — Compound Annual Growth Rate

The smoothed annual growth rate over a period. CAGR = (End / Start)^(1/years) − 1 Why it matters: standardizes multi-year growth into one comparable number. ↩ back to Section 2

P/S — Price-to-Sales Ratio

Market cap divided by revenue. P/S = Market Cap / Revenue FTA scale: >30 Euphoric · >18 Excited · >8 Optimistic · 4–8 Aware · <4 Cautious · <2 Pessimistic. Why it matters: usable when a company has no earnings; ASTS's ~180x is off-the-charts Euphoric. ↩ back to Section 2

P/E — Price-to-Earnings Ratio

Price per share divided by earnings per share. P/E = Price / EPS Why it matters: the classic valuation yardstick — but meaningless ("n/m") for a loss-making company like ASTS. ↩ back to Section 2

Real Option / Optionality

Valuing a business as the probability-weighted payoff of a future opportunity, akin to a call option, rather than on current cash flows. Why it matters: the only coherent way to value a pre-revenue, binary-outcome company — most of ASTS's value is contingent future cash flow. ↩ back to Section 2

FCF — Free Cash Flow

Cash from operations minus capital expenditures — the cash a business actually generates after investment. FCF = Operating Cash Flow − CapEx Why it matters: for a capital-heavy build like AST's, FCF burn vs. cash on hand defines the runway and dilution risk. ↩ back to Section 4
