Executive Summary & Investment Thesis

▾
TeraWulf is no longer a Bitcoin miner — it is becoming a "power landlord of AI," converting cheap, low-carbon power and shovel-ready land into long-dated, contracted AI data-center leases. In Q1 2026, for the first time ever, HPC lease revenue ($21M) overtook Bitcoin mining. With ~$12.8B of contracted revenue across 522 critical-IT megawatts — and Google backstopping ~$3.2B+ of the lease obligations in exchange for a ~14% equity stake — the market is mid-way through re-rating WULF from a volatile mining multiple to a digital-infrastructure multiple. The asymmetry is real, and so is the single-counterparty risk.

The bull case is not subtle: a sub-$13B-cap company holds a contracted revenue backlog roughly equal to its entire market value, anchored by one of the most creditworthy backstops on earth. If WULF executes its year-end 2026 buildout, today's ~$130M revenue run-rate is a rounding error against the contracted ramp. The catch — and it is a serious one — is that Fluidstack represents ~91% of contracted revenue, the company is still deeply loss-making on a GAAP basis, and the equity trades at a nosebleed ~53x forward EV/EBITDA. This is a high-variance, binary-flavored bet best sized as such.

Microeconomic & Valuation Analysis

▾

Industry & TAM

WULF sits at the intersection of two scarcities: power and shovel-ready data-center capacity. AI compute demand is bottlenecked not by chips alone but by gigawatts of interconnected, cooled, powered shell — the "neocloud" build-out. Bernstein, initiating coverage of the miners-turned-landlords, framed the group as "the power landlords of AI" and modeled ~9x AI revenue growth by 2030. The relevant TAM is the multi-hundred-billion-dollar annual AI data-center capex cycle, where the binding constraint is exactly what WULF owns: large, permitted, low-carbon power sites at Lake Mariner (NY) and Abernathy (TX).

Qualitative Analysis — Moat, Management & Culture

▾

The Moat — a "power + permits + counterparty" moat, not a tech moat

WULF has no proprietary technology moat; its edge is positional. The scarce, defensible assets are (1) large interconnected power capacity at Lake Mariner, predominantly low-carbon (nuclear/hydro-adjacent) grid power — a genuine ESG and cost advantage; (2) permitted, buildable land that takes years and political capital for rivals to replicate; and (3) a creditworthy anchor in Google's backstop, which lowers WULF's cost of project debt below what an unbacked miner could ever achieve. The moat is therefore a flywheel of financing advantage: Google's backstop → cheaper debt → faster buildout → more contracted MW → more backstop. It is real but rests on relationships and contracts rather than switching costs.

Management & capital allocation

WULF is founder-led by Chairman & CEO Paul Prager, an energy-infrastructure veteran whose power-development background is the company's DNA — appropriate for a business whose core asset is power, not silicon. Capital allocation has pivoted decisively: away from bitcoin balance-sheet accumulation and toward non-dilutive, project-level financing of contracted data centers, plus the sale of non-core assets (e.g., the Nautilus mining stake). The strategic clarity is a positive; the execution bar is now enormous.

Culture & organizational traits

Culturally WULF behaves like an energy developer that learned to mine bitcoin, not a tech firm that bought power. That orientation — patient site development, low-carbon emphasis, deal-structuring sophistication — is well suited to the AI-power moment. The organizational watch-item is the leap from operating ~60 MW of HPC today to delivering 500+ MW of contracted capacity on tight timelines: that is a construction- and operations-execution muscle the company is still building, with heavy reliance on partners.

Moat verdict: Durable on power/land/financing; thin on technology and dangerously thin on counterparty diversification. The moat is the megawatts and the Google relationship — not anything that can't be competed away if power becomes abundant.

Cash, balance sheet & the burn

WULF ended Q1 2026 with ~$3.1B of cash and restricted cash and closed a $250M revolving credit facility — an unusually strong liquidity position for a company this size, reflecting capital raised to fund the buildout. The flip side: free cash flow is deeply negative as the company pours capital into construction, and the trailing GAAP result is a sizeable net loss (TTM net loss ~$0.56B through late 2025, inflated by non-cash items). This is a build-now, earn-later profile: cash is being consumed to create the contracted earning assets that the bull case monetizes from 2027 onward.

Risks & Anti-Thesis — Steelmanning the Bear

▾

If I were short WULF, the case writes itself — and Bernstein, a bull, flags the first risk explicitly.

1. Catastrophic counterparty concentration (Fluidstack ~91%)

Roughly 91% of contracted revenue rests on a single tenant, Fluidstack — a relatively young AI-cloud company. Google backstops the lease obligations, not WULF's equity, and a Fluidstack failure or renegotiation would be deeply disruptive even if the backstop limits credit loss. One customer is the whole story.

2. Execution & construction risk

Going from ~60 MW live to 500+ MW on tight 2026 timelines is an enormous build. Power-interconnect delays, equipment lead times, and cost overruns are the norm, not the exception, in data-center construction. Any slip pushes revenue right and pressures the multiple.

Prob: HighImpact: HighPriced in: No

3. Valuation prices the backlog as if it's already cash

At ~53x forward EV/EBITDA and a market cap roughly equal to undelivered contracted revenue, the equity discounts near-flawless conversion. GAAP losses persist. Any wobble in the AI-capex narrative could compress this richly-valued, high-beta name violently.

Prob: MediumImpact: SeverePriced in: No

4. Dilution & financing dependence

The buildout is capital-hungry. Between Google's ~73.5M warrant shares, potential ATM equity, and project debt, the share count and leverage can keep climbing. Per-share value depends on financing the ramp without excessive dilution — not guaranteed.

Prob: MediumImpact: MediumPriced in: Partially

5. AI-power glut / commoditization

The entire thesis rests on power scarcity. If the wave of announced data-center capacity (hyperscalers, neoclouds, sovereigns) actually gets built, powered shell could shift from scarce to abundant, compressing lease economics and renewal pricing across the group.

Prob: MediumImpact: HighPriced in: No
Anti-thesis synthesis: WULF is a leveraged, single-tenant, pre-profit construction story dressed as an infrastructure compounder. The Google backstop is real risk mitigation, but it does not eliminate execution, concentration, or valuation risk. Position sizing — not conviction in the narrative — is what protects you here.

First-Principles KPIs

▾

Forget GAAP EPS for now. Four leading indicators tell you whether this transformation is on track before the income statement catches up:

  1. Energized critical-IT MW — the single truest gauge of execution. Contracted MW only matter once they're live and billing (60 MW today → 522 MW contracted).
  2. HPC lease revenue & its share of total — proof the mining-to-infrastructure pivot is converting to recurring cash ($9.7M → $21M in one quarter; HPC now > mining).
  3. Tenant concentration — Fluidstack's % of contracted revenue. Falling = de-risking; the most important number for the multiple.
  4. Cost of capital / financing terms — the Google-backstop flywheel only works if each tranche of project debt prices attractively.

Glossary & Methodology

▾
Neocloud
A new class of specialized AI cloud/data-center providers (e.g., CoreWeave, Fluidstack) built specifically for GPU compute, distinct from the big-three hyperscalers. Why it matters: WULF leases capacity to neoclouds; their demand and creditworthiness drive WULF's revenue. ↩ back
EV/EBITDA
Enterprise value (market cap + debt − cash) ÷ EBITDA. NTM = next-twelve-months forward estimate. WULF ≈ 53x NTM. Why it matters: the only sensible multiple for a pre-profit, capital-intensive infrastructure name; it captures debt and forward cash earnings, not trailing losses. ↩ back
P/S — Price-to-Sales
Market cap ÷ revenue. Against WULF's tiny current revenue this is astronomically high ("Euphoric" on the house scale, >30). Why it matters: it shows why current financials can't justify the price — the bet is on contracted future revenue, not today's sales. ↩ back
FCF — Free Cash Flow
Operating cash flow minus capital expenditures; currently deeply negative as WULF builds. Why it matters: a build-now/earn-later profile means FCF turns positive only after contracted capacity is energized — the central timing risk of the thesis. ↩ back
Critical-IT MW
Megawatts of power actually delivered to computing equipment (net of cooling/overhead), the standard data-center capacity unit. WULF: 522 MW contracted, ~60 MW live. Why it matters: it's the real measure of how much revenue-generating capacity exists vs is merely promised.
Backstop
A guarantee by a creditworthy party (here, Google) of another company's (Fluidstack's) lease obligations, supporting WULF's project debt. Why it matters: it lowers WULF's cost of capital and de-risks the contracted revenue — the linchpin of the bull case.
