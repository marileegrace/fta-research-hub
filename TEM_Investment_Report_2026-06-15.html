Executive Summary & Investment Thesis

▼
Tempus AI is mispriced as a clinical lab company when its durable edge is a self-reinforcing data flywheel. Each diagnostic test the market values on reimbursement economics simultaneously enriches a proprietary multimodal oncology dataset that pharma and AI developers pay recurring, high-margin dollars to access. The diagnostics business funds data capture; data monetization is where operating leverage and the real moat live. As the company crosses into Adjusted EBITDA profitability in 2026, the equity should re-rate from "story stock" to "compounding data platform."

Tempus closed Q1 2026 with $348.1M of revenue, up 36.1% year-over-year, its Data & Applications segment growing 40.5% and posting a third consecutive quarter of bookings above $100M. Adjusted EBITDA improved to –$2.8M from –$16.2M a year earlier, and management raised full-year revenue guidance to $1.59–1.60B with ~$65M of Adjusted EBITDA. The trajectory toward profitability is no longer hypothetical — it is being delivered quarter by quarter.

The bear's strongest point is honest: TEM still posts large GAAP losses (FY2025 net loss of –$245M), carries ~$930M of debt and convertibles, dilutes shareholders through heavy stock-based compensation, and trades at a premium to slower-growing diagnostics peers. The Q1 2026 GAAP EPS of –$0.71 badly missed the –$0.10 consensus. This is not a clean compounder yet. The bull case rests on the data segment's margin profile and the founder's history of building category-defining businesses.

Microeconomic & Valuation Analysis

▼

Industry & TAM

Tempus sits at the intersection of three expanding markets: molecular/genomic oncology diagnostics, real-world clinical-genomic data licensing, and AI-enabled clinical decision tools. Management frames a combined addressable opportunity well north of $100B; the oncology diagnostics slice alone is commonly sized in the $40–60B range and the precision-medicine data/AI layer is growing faster off a smaller base. Industry CAGR estimates for clinical genomic testing cluster around 12–18%, while real-world-data and health-AI markets are frequently modeled at 20–30%+ — meaning TEM's revenue mix is tilting toward the faster-growing layers.

Valuation

With a market cap of roughly $8.6B and trailing-twelve-month revenue near $1.34B, TEM trades at a P/S of ~6.4x trailing and ~5.4x on the $1.6B 2026 guide. On the report's P/S scale that sits in the "Aware" band (4–8) — neither euphoric nor cheap. There is no meaningful P/E because the company is not yet GAAP-profitable; valuation must rest on revenue growth, gross-margin trajectory, and the path to free cash flow.

Qualitative Analysis — Moat, Management & Culture

▼

The Moat — A Data Flywheel, Philosophically

The most defensible moats are systems that get harder to replicate the more they are used. Tempus's moat is exactly this: every sequenced tumor, paired with longitudinal clinical outcomes across a network of academic and community oncology sites, deepens a dataset that is impossible to buy off a shelf. More tests → richer multimodal data → more valuable to pharma and AI partners → more revenue to fund more testing and partnerships. The asset is not the algorithm or the assay (both copyable); it is the compounding linkage between molecular data and real-world outcomes at scale, governed by consent and contracts that a new entrant cannot retroactively assemble.

Systems View
A competitor can build a better single test. It cannot build a longer history. Tempus's edge is path-dependent — it accrues with time and connectivity, the two things money alone cannot purchase.

Management & Capital Allocation

Tempus is founder-led and founder-controlled. Eric Lefkofsky (CEO/Chairman since founding in 2015) is a serial category-builder — Groupon, Echo Global Logistics, InnerWorkings, Mediaocean, and venture firm Lightbank. That track record cuts both ways: deep operating range and credibility raising capital, but also a history of aggressive growth-first execution. The 2025 Ambry Genetics acquisition (~$692M) was a sizeable bet that immediately added hereditary-testing scale and accelerated revenue to ~80% growth for the year — a capital-allocation move that has so far compounded the data and distribution base.

Culture & Organizational Traits

Tempus exhibits classic high-velocity health-tech traits: rapid product iteration (xT/xF/xR assay families, MRD, the Lens data platform), high tolerance for unprofitable investment in pursuit of category leadership, and strong talent density at the engineering/data-science intersection of medicine. The structural risk is the flip side of founder control — concentrated decision rights and elevated stock-based compensation that transfers value from public shareholders to insiders and employees. Investors are effectively betting on idea-meritocracy and innovation pace outrunning dilution.

Quantitative Analysis — Financial Deep Dive

▼

The financial story is one of rapid top-line growth converging toward profitability, funded today by the balance sheet rather than by operations. FY2025 revenue reached ~$1.25B (~80% growth, boosted by the Ambry acquisition), with a net loss of –$245M that included $136.3M of stock-based compensation and related payroll taxes — i.e., a large chunk of the loss is non-cash. Adjusted EBITDA improved by $97.3M YoY to –$7.4M for 2025, and Q1 2026 narrowed to –$2.8M, with full-year 2026 guided to ~+$65M. That is the inflection the thesis hinges on.

Revenue Streams, Explained Simply

Diagnostics ($261.1M in Q1'26, +34.7%): Tempus runs molecular tests — primarily oncology (tumor sequencing, ~28% unit growth), plus hereditary testing from Ambry and minimal-residual-disease (MRD) testing. Think of this as the "razor": each test generates revenue and, crucially, data. Data & Applications ($87.0M, +40.5%): Tempus licenses de-identified multimodal datasets and AI tools to pharma and researchers (the "Insights" business grew 44.1%). This is the "blades" — higher margin, recurring, and capital-light.

Balance Sheet, Cash & Dilution

Tempus held $643.8M in cash and marketable securities at 31 Mar 2026 (down from $759.7M at year-end 2025 as it funds operations and working capital). Debt comprises roughly $203M of term loan (net), $728M of convertible senior notes (net), and a $100M revolver — meaningful leverage for a not-yet-FCF-positive company, though the converts push maturities out and the cash balance provides multi-year runway at the current burn. Weighted-average diluted shares were ~174M in 2025; ongoing equity compensation is the principal dilution vector to monitor.

Risks & Anti-Thesis (Steelman the Bear)

▼

If I were short TEM, here is the case I would press — engaged seriously, not as a strawman.

1. The company has never generated GAAP profit or sustained free cash flow

FY2025 net loss was –$245M and Q1'26 EPS missed badly at –$0.71. Adjusted EBITDA flatters the picture by excluding $130M+ of annual stock comp that is a real economic cost to shareholders. "Path to profitability" has been the pitch for years.

Probability: HighImpact: HighPriced in: Partially

2. Diagnostics is reimbursement-gated and competitive

Test pricing depends on payer and CMS decisions outside the company's control. MRD reimbursement is "progressing" but not locked. Guardant, Natera, Exact Sciences and others compete fiercely on price and clinical evidence, capping diagnostics margin.

Probability: Medium-HighImpact: MediumPriced in: Partially

3. The data thesis is concentrated and lumpy

Data & Applications revenue depends on a finite set of large pharma contracts. Bookings can be lumpy; a few delayed or non-renewed deals would puncture the "recurring high-margin" narrative the bull case relies on.

Probability: MediumImpact: HighPriced in: No

4. Dilution and a levered balance sheet

~$930M of debt/converts plus persistent stock issuance. If equity markets tighten or growth slows before FCF turns positive, refinancing and dilution risk rises sharply — a classic squeeze for cash-burning growth names.

Probability: MediumImpact: HighPriced in: Partially

5. Founder control concentrates governance risk

Super-voting structure means public holders have limited say on capital allocation, M&A (e.g., another Ambry-scale deal), or compensation. Aligned today; a liability if priorities ever diverge from minority shareholders.

Probability: Low-MediumImpact: MediumPriced in: No
Anti-Thesis Verdict
The bear case is coherent and the EPS miss is a genuine yellow flag. It is rebuttable only if the data segment keeps compounding at 40%+ and the 2026 EBITDA target is met. Miss those, and the bear wins.

Glossary & Methodology

▼

Price-to-Sales (P/S) — Market cap ÷ trailing-twelve-month revenue. Formula: P/S = Market Cap / Revenue. Why it matters: the primary valuation tool for unprofitable growth companies where P/E is meaningless. Scale used here: >30 Euphoric, >18 Excited, >8 Optimistic, 4–8 Aware, <4 Cautious, <2 Pessimistic. ↩ back to §2

Price-to-Earnings (P/E) — Price ÷ earnings per share. Why it matters: standard profitability multiple; not meaningful for TEM because it is not yet GAAP-profitable. ↩ back to §2

Adjusted EBITDA — Earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, amortization, and adjustments (notably stock-based comp). Why it matters: a proxy for cash operating profitability, but it excludes real shareholder costs like SBC — read it alongside GAAP. ↩ back to §1

Free Cash Flow (FCF) — Operating cash flow minus capital expenditures. Formula: FCF = OCF − CapEx. Why it matters: the truest measure of self-funding; the milestone that would end dilution dependence. ↩ back to §2

EPS — Net income (loss) ÷ diluted shares. Why it matters: headline profitability; TEM's GAAP EPS is distorted by large non-cash charges, so revenue and EBITDA are more informative here. ↩ back to §1

Stock-Based Compensation (SBC) — Equity paid to employees, expensed on the income statement. Why it matters: non-cash but dilutive — it transfers ownership from existing holders. $136M+ in FY25. ↩ back to §1

Dilution — The reduction in existing holders' ownership as new shares are issued. Why it matters: for cash-burning growth companies, even great operating results can be offset by a rising share count. ↩ back to §4

Data & Applications / Insights — Tempus's segment licensing de-identified multimodal clinical-genomic data and AI tools to pharma and researchers. Why it matters: the high-margin, capital-light engine at the center of the bull thesis. ↩ back to §1

CAGR — Compound Annual Growth Rate. Formula: (End/Start)^(1/years) − 1. Why it matters: smooths multi-year growth into a single comparable rate for TAM and revenue. ↩ back to §2
