Executive Summary & Investment Thesis

▾
Rocket Lab is the rare second mover that can credibly challenge an entrenched monopolist. We are constructive bulls on a generational, founder-led franchise — but the stock at ~97x sales already discounts a near-flawless Neutron debut. The asymmetry favors accumulating on weakness rather than chasing strength.

Rocket Lab has quietly become the most complete pure-play in the public space economy. It is the only company outside SpaceX that simultaneously operates the world's most frequently launched small orbital rocket (Electron), a fast-scaling space systems manufacturing arm (satellites, components, solar, propulsion), and a credible medium-lift, partially reusable vehicle in late-stage development (Neutron). Q1 2026 delivered record revenue of $200.3M (+63.5% YoY), a record $2.2B backlog, and record GAAP gross margin of 38.2% — beating every guided metric.

The bull thesis rests on three pillars: (1) Neutron unlocks the ~10x-larger medium-lift and constellation-deployment market, structurally re-rating the launch segment; (2) Golden Dome / Space-Based Interceptor with Raytheon positions Rocket Lab inside what could become the U.S. government's single largest national-security program; and (3) vertical integration (Mynaric optical comms, Motiv robotics, in-housed propulsion) compounds margin and supply control as the space systems business scales toward a prime-contractor profile.

The bear's strongest card is valuation and timing. At roughly $66B market cap on ~$679M trailing revenue, RKLB trades near 97x sales — a number that only makes sense if Neutron flies, scales, and lands reuse on a 2027–2029 cadence while the company crosses into sustained free-cash-flow generation. The January 2026 fuel-tank test failure and slip of the Neutron debut to Q4 2026 (at the earliest) is a reminder that the entire re-rating hinges on a vehicle that has not yet reached orbit. Our conviction of 7/10 reflects a world-class franchise we want to own, tempered by a price that demands the bull case to be largely correct.

Qualitative Analysis — Moat, Management & Culture

▾

The Moat — Systemic, Not Singular

Rocket Lab's moat is best understood philosophically as a compounding integration loop rather than a single defensible feature. Each capability it brings in-house — reaction wheels, star trackers, solar arrays, separation systems, flight software, now optical comms (Mynaric) and robotics/precision mechanisms (Motiv) — simultaneously lowers its own cost, removes a supply-chain bottleneck, and creates a merchant-supplier revenue stream sold to competitors. The result is a flywheel where vertical integration funds R&D, R&D widens the product catalog, the catalog wins more missions, and more missions amortize the integration. SpaceX is the only other company running this loop at scale, and it is not investable directly.

The deeper moat is reliability-as-reputation. Electron is the most-launched small orbital rocket in the world; that flight heritage is a trust asset that cannot be bought, only accumulated launch by launch. In national security, where Rocket Lab is now embedded in Golden Dome's Space-Based Interceptor with Raytheon, that heritage plus a U.S.-soil, vertically controlled supply chain is precisely what buyers underwrite.

Management & Capital Allocation

Founder-CEO Peter Beck is the defining variable. He built the company from New Zealand garage origins to a vertically integrated, two-continent space prime, and is one of the few launch-industry leaders to bring a new orbital rocket to operational cadence and keep it there. Capital allocation has been aggressive but coherent: a $450M ATM raise and convertible-note management built >$2B of liquidity, deployed into Neutron, the Mynaric close, and the pending Motiv acquisition. The strategy is to weaponize a richly valued currency to vertically integrate and buy capability — sensible while the multiple holds, riskier if the stock de-rates mid-build.

Culture & Organizational Traits

Rocket Lab exhibits the structural traits the best engineering organizations share: high talent density concentrated around hard propulsion and avionics problems, a bias toward insourcing over outsourcing (decentralized capability ownership), and a publicly demonstrated failure tolerance — the January 2026 fuel-tank rupture during stress testing was framed by management as a deliberate test-to-failure to understand structural margins, then engineered around. That is the signature of an organization that treats failure as information rather than embarrassment, the same cultural DNA that underpins iterative aerospace development. Innovation pace is high: in a single quarter the company introduced the Gauss electric thruster, won a record MACH-TB hypersonic contract, signed 31 Electron/HASTE contracts plus five Neutron launches, and closed/announced two acquisitions.

Moat verdict: Wide and widening on the systems side; narrowing the gap on launch. The single-point dependency is Beck and the Neutron engineering org — a key-person and key-program concentration that is both the moat and the risk.

The Flywheel

Revenue compounding (FY22 $211M → FY23 $245M → FY24 $436M → FY25 $602M, a ~76% five-year CAGR) feeds a backlog that has grown 73% YoY to $1.85B at year-end and on to $2.2B in Q1'26. Backlog converts into revenue, revenue funds vertical integration, integration lifts margins, margins fund Neutron, and Neutron expands the addressable launch market — closing the loop at a larger scale each turn. The key unproven link is the conversion of Neutron backlog into flown, paid, repeatable missions.

Risks & Anti-Thesis — Steelman the Bear

▾

If we were short RKLB, here is the case we would press. Each risk is rated on probability, impact, and how much we believe is already priced in.

1. Neutron is a clean-sheet, reusable rocket that has never flown

The entire valuation premium rests on a vehicle that failed a fuel-tank stress test in January 2026 and has slipped to Q4'26 at the earliest. New orbital rockets routinely fail on debut; reusability adds another order of magnitude of difficulty. A second material delay or a launch failure would vaporize the optionality premium overnight.

First-Principles KPIs

▾

Forget the income statement for a moment. The leading indicators that actually determine whether the bull thesis compounds are operational, not accounting. We track four.

1 · Backlog & Backlog Coverage

Backlog ($2.2B, +20% QoQ) is the truest forward demand signal — it grew faster than revenue, meaning bookings are outrunning deliveries. Backlog ÷ TTM revenue of ~3.2x is healthy multi-year coverage. Watch the launch-vs-systems mix (currently ~42% / 58%) — Neutron wins shifting the mix toward launch would confirm medium-lift demand.

2 · Launch Cadence & Manifest Depth

The manifest now exceeds 70 contracted missions, and the company sold more launches in Q1'26 than in all of FY2025. Cadence is the flywheel's RPM — each incremental launch amortizes fixed costs and compounds reliability heritage.

3 · Gross Margin Expansion

GAAP gross margin climbed from ~28.8% (Q1'25) to 38.2% (Q1'26). This is the single cleanest proof of operating leverage and vertical-integration payoff. The path to FCF positivity runs directly through this line.

4 · Net Cash Runway

~$1.42B net cash funds the Neutron build and M&A without forced dilution at depressed prices. Runway is the survival metric — it is why Rocket Lab, unlike prior launch entrants, can afford to be late.

Glossary & Methodology

▾
Electron
Rocket Lab's small orbital launch vehicle — the most frequently launched small rocket in the world. Why it matters: the flight-heritage and reliability asset that anchors the company's reputation and recurring launch revenue. ↩ back to §1
Neutron
Rocket Lab's in-development medium-lift, partially reusable launch vehicle, targeting constellation deployment and national-security missions. Debut targeted for Q4 2026 at the earliest. Why it matters: unlocks a ~10x-larger addressable launch market and is the core of the valuation's optionality. ↩ back to §1
Space Systems
The product segment: satellites, spacecraft buses, solar arrays, reaction wheels, star trackers, separation systems, propulsion, and optical comms. Why it matters: now ~64% of revenue and the larger growth engine; sells components to competitors, widening the moat. ↩ back to §1
Backlog
Contracted but not-yet-recognized revenue ($2.2B in Q1'26). Formula: sum of remaining performance obligations across launch + systems contracts. Why it matters: the cleanest leading indicator of future revenue and demand momentum. ↩ back to §4
CAGR (Compound Annual Growth Rate)
Formula: (Ending ÷ Beginning)^(1/years) − 1. Why it matters: smooths multi-year growth into a single comparable rate; RKLB's ~76% 5-yr revenue CAGR signals exceptional top-line compounding. ↩ back to §2
P/S (Price-to-Sales) Ratio
Formula: Market Cap ÷ TTM Revenue. FTA scale: >30 Euphoric, >18 Excited, >8 Optimistic, 4–8 Aware, <4 Cautious, <2 Pessimistic. Why it matters: the primary valuation lens for pre-profit growth names where P/E is meaningless. RKLB at ~97x is deep in Euphoric territory. ↩ back to §2
ATM (At-the-Market Offering)
A mechanism to sell new shares into the open market over time at prevailing prices ($450M raised in Q1'26). Why it matters: low-friction equity funding that supports growth but dilutes existing holders. ↩ back to §2
Free Cash Flow (FCF)
Formula: Operating Cash Flow − Capital Expenditures (Q1'26: −$50.3M − $27.1M ≈ −$77M). Why it matters: the ultimate measure of self-funding; the trajectory toward positive FCF is the key profitability milestone. ↩ back to §4
Operating Leverage
The degree to which revenue growth outpaces cost growth, expanding margins. Why it matters: RKLB's gross profit grew +117% YoY versus revenue +63%, evidence that scale is converting into margin. ↩ back to §4
Adjusted EBITDA
Earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, amortization, and items management deems non-representative (e.g., SBC, transaction costs). Q1'26: −$11.8M, improving from −$30.0M. Why it matters: a proxy for underlying cash operating performance, smoothing non-cash and one-time items. ↩ back to §4
