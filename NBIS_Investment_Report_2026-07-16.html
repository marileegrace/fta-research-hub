Executive Summary & Investment Thesis

▼
Nebius is not a "GPU landlord" — it is a full-stack cloud software company that happens to own the metal. The market prices NBIS like a commodity capex machine, yet its differentiated orchestration layer, Nvidia sponsorship, ~$46B of contracted backlog and a newly-announced asset-light partner model give it hyperscaler-grade economics at a neocloud valuation. Today's ~13% neocloud-wide sell-off is macro noise, not thesis damage.

Nebius Group emerged from the 2024 breakup of Yandex as a clean, Amsterdam-domiciled, Nasdaq-listed entity holding the international AI-infrastructure assets, ~$2.5B of starting cash and Nvidia + Accel as anchor investors. Eighteen months later it is arguably the fastest-scaling infrastructure business on public markets: Q1 2026 revenue of $399M grew 684% year-on-year, with the core AI-cloud segment up 841% and ARR reaching $1.92B — up 54% in a single quarter.

Management guides FY2026 to $3.0–3.4B revenue, $7–9B exit ARR and ~40% adjusted EBITDA margin, funded by $9.3B of cash and a raised capex plan of $20–25B against >4GW of contracted power. The anchor is a five-year, $17.4B Microsoft contract, layered with Meta, a fresh $1B Reflection AI deal and dozens of smaller enterprise wins.

Microeconomic & Valuation Analysis

▼

The Market Being Contested

Nebius sits in the "neocloud" layer — specialised GPU cloud providers wedged between Nvidia's silicon and the hyperscalers' general-purpose clouds. Independent estimates put AI-infrastructure / accelerated-compute spend on a trajectory from roughly $150B in 2025 toward $400B+ by 2030, a CAGR north of 20%. Neoclouds are the fastest-growing slice because hyperscalers cannot build capacity fast enough and increasingly rent from third parties (see Microsoft ↔ Nebius, Meta ↔ CoreWeave).

Qualitative — Moat, Management & Culture

▼

The Moat — Systemic, Not Just Physical

The lazy bear case is "GPUs are a commodity; anyone with capital can rent Nvidia chips." That misreads where Nebius's durability lives. Three reinforcing layers:

  • Software / orchestration. Nebius is one of the only neoclouds built on a fully in-house cloud stack (inherited and hardened from Yandex's hyperscale heritage). Its AI Cloud 3.x platform, managed SkyPilot orchestration, the new "Echo" agent, KMS security and storage tiering are the switching-cost layer that pure GPU-rental shops lack.
  • Nvidia sponsorship & allocation. As an Nvidia Cloud Partner with a strategic investment, Nebius gets preferential access to the newest silicon (GB300 access is being democratized to customers). In a supply-constrained world, allocation is the moat.
  • The asset-light flywheel (new, July 2026). Nebius just unveiled a model letting infrastructure partners deploy Nebius's full software stack inside their own data centers — converting a capex-heavy business into a higher-margin, faster-scaling platform license. This is the "Shopify-for-GPU-clouds" optionality the market has not yet priced.

Management & Founder Alignment

Nebius is founder-led: Arkady Volozh, the co-founder and former CEO of Yandex — one of the few non-US tech founders to build a genuine hyperscaler from scratch — is CEO and a major shareholder. This is a builder with two decades of operating a search-and-cloud business at scale, now pointed entirely at AI infrastructure. Capital allocation has been aggressive but disciplined: raise ahead of demand, lock multi-year take-or-pay contracts before pouring concrete, and monetise non-core stakes (ClickHouse, Toloka, Avride, TripleTen) to fund the core.

Cultural signal: Nebius ships fast (multiple platform releases per quarter, an annual AI Discovery Awards program, a developer Builder Program) and tolerates the messiness of hyper-scaling. Talent density is high — this is essentially the surviving engineering core of one of the world's few independent hyperscalers, re-domiciled and re-pointed.

Organizational traits: high innovation pace, strong customer-obsession in the developer motion, and a decentralised, idea-meritocratic engineering culture. Structural risk: a single dominant founder and a still-thin (~1,543 employee) org scaling into a multi-GW build — execution depends heavily on a small leadership bench.

Revenue Streams — In Plain English

Nebius earns money four ways, but one dominates. (1) AI Cloud — renting GPU compute, storage and its software platform to AI labs and enterprises; this is ~98% of revenue and the whole thesis. What the Numbers Say

The signal that matters most: the core AI-cloud segment turned adjusted-EBITDA positive in Q2 2025 and kept improving, even as the consolidated business burns cash. That is textbook operating leverage — revenue is scaling far faster than the fixed cost base, and management guides the group to ~40% adjusted EBITDA margin at FY2026 scale.

The obvious offset is free cash flow: -$3.7B in FY2025 and going more negative as capex ramps to $20–25B. This is deliberate, contract-backed capacity building, not undisciplined spending — but it makes NBIS dependent on continued access to capital markets. The $9.3B cash pile (~$7B unrestricted + ~$2.3B customer prepayments) plus the asset-light pivot are how management intends to bend the capex curve.

The flywheel: Contracted power → GPU deployment → ARR → EBITDA → reinvestment + cheaper capital → more contracted power. Asset-light partnerships add a second, capital-light loop on top. Each turn lowers cost per MW ($0.94M revenue per active MW today) and widens the moat.

Risks & Anti-Thesis (Steelman the Bear)

▼

If I were short NBIS, here is the case I would build — and I take it seriously.

1 · AI-capex digestion / neocloud oversupply

Probability: Medium-HighImpact: SeverePriced in: Partly

The entire sector is spending hundreds of billions ahead of demand. If enterprises "valuemaxx" and slow AI budgets — or if GPU supply catches up — spot compute pricing collapses and NBIS's take-or-pay contracts become the only thing standing between it and a margin implosion. Today's ~13% drop shows how violently the group re-rates on this fear.

2 · Customer concentration & the Microsoft/Meta dependency

Probability: MediumImpact: SeverePriced in: No

A huge share of contracted backlog rests on a handful of hyperscaler counterparties. Microsoft is both anchor customer and potential future competitor; Meta has signalled it may resell its own excess compute, directly threatening neocloud demand. Loss or non-renewal of one anchor would gut the ARR bridge.

3 · Capital intensity & dilution treadmill

Probability: HighImpact: Moderate-HighPriced in: Partly

-$3.7B FCF in FY25 with $20–25B of capex ahead. Shares already rose ~30% in 18 months. Even with $9.3B cash, funding a multi-GW build almost certainly means more equity, converts or debt — each raise caps per-share upside and adds refinancing risk if rates or sentiment turn.

4 · Nvidia dependency cuts both ways

Probability: MediumImpact: ModeratePriced in: No

Preferential allocation is today's moat, but it is a relationship, not a contract-for-life. "Circular financing" critiques (Nvidia invests in customers who buy Nvidia chips) invite scrutiny; any shift in Nvidia's neocloud strategy, export-control changes, or a competing accelerator wave would erode NBIS's edge.

5 · Valuation leaves no room for error

Probability: HighImpact: ModeratePriced in: Yes

At ~14x forward sales the stock discounts near-flawless execution. Any ARR-pace miss, margin slippage, regulatory data-center friction (e.g. the New York order rattling the group today), or a single soft guide can trigger 20–40% drawdowns — as the 52-week range from $49 to $300 already demonstrates.

Anti-thesis synthesis: The bear case is real and the volatility is the price of admission. What tips me bullish: the contracts are take-or-pay, the software/asset-light layer is a genuine differentiator, the balance sheet is strong, and the core segment is already EBITDA-positive — so this is not a pre-revenue story hoping for a miracle. Position small, expect drawdowns, hold the thesis.

Relative Read

On trailing P/S, NBIS is the most expensive neocloud — but it is also the fastest-growing by a wide margin (+684% vs CoreWeave's +112%) and the only one leaning hard into a software and asset-light differentiation, which should command a premium multiple. CoreWeave is the scale leader (~$5B revenue, ~$100B backlog) and screens "cheaper" at ~8x sales, making it the natural pair-trade hedge. IREN offers a power-first angle. Against Nvidia — its supplier and backer — Nebius is the leveraged downstream play: it wins if AI-compute demand keeps outrunning supply.

Takeaway: The premium is defensible only if the software/asset-light narrative converts to margin. If NBIS becomes "just another GPU renter," it should trade toward CoreWeave's ~8x — the crux of the bull/bear divide.

Glossary & Methodology

▼

ARR — Annualized Recurring Revenue

ARR ≈ latest-month recurring revenue × 12

The forward run-rate of contracted, recurring revenue. Why it matters: for a business scaling this fast, ARR is a far better read of true size than trailing revenue — it captures capacity that just came online. NBIS core ARR = $1.92B (Q1'26), targeted to $7–9B exit 2026.

↩ back to summary

CAGR — Compound Annual Growth Rate

CAGR = (End / Start)^(1/years) − 1

The smoothed annual growth rate over a multi-year period. Why it matters: used here to frame the ~20%+ AI-infrastructure TAM growth and to compare Nebius's revenue trajectory against the market it serves.

↩ back to valuation

P/S — Price-to-Sales Ratio

P/S = Market Cap ÷ Revenue

Valuation vs revenue, used when earnings are not yet meaningful. Sentiment scale used in this report: >30 Euphoric · >18 Excited · 8–18 Optimistic · 4–8 Aware · <4 Cautious · <2 Pessimistic. NBIS: ~51x trailing (Euphoric) but ~14x forward (Excited).

↩ back to valuation

Free Cash Flow (FCF)

FCF = Operating Cash Flow − Capital Expenditure

Cash left after funding the business and its growth investments. Why it matters: NBIS is deeply FCF-negative (-$3.7B FY25) by design — building capacity ahead of demand. The bull case requires this to inflect positive as ARR scales; the bear case is that it never does.

↩ back to financials

Operating Leverage

% change in EBITDA ÷ % change in revenue (>1 = leverage)

The degree to which fixed costs let profit grow faster than revenue. Why it matters: Nebius's core segment flipped from -$129M to +$59M adj. EBITDA while revenue multiplied — direct evidence the model has real operating leverage.

↩ back to financials

EV / EBITDA — Enterprise Value to EBITDA

EV = Mkt Cap + Debt − Cash; ÷ EBITDA

Capital-structure-neutral valuation. Why it matters: not yet meaningful for NBIS (consolidated EBITDA still negative), which is exactly why the report leans on forward P/S and P/ARR instead.

↩ back to comps

Neocloud

A specialised cloud provider offering GPU-dense infrastructure purpose-built for AI training and inference, distinct from general-purpose hyperscalers. Peers: CoreWeave, IREN, Crusoe, Lambda. Why it matters: the category NBIS competes in — high growth, high capex, contested by hyperscalers moving in the opposite direction.

↩ back to valuation

Take-or-Pay Contract

A commitment where the customer pays for reserved capacity whether or not it is used. Why it matters: these underpin Nebius's ~$46B backlog and are the shock-absorber if spot GPU pricing falls — the reason the bull case survives an oversupply scare.
